01The Promise

Built to serve generations of Quilters.

A Gammill is designed for a shockingly long life. The Gammill machine you order today will outlast all 8 billion humans on earth. It will bring joy to many generations of Quilters.

All Gammill machines since 1997 are functioning today unless they were lost to fire, flood, or other external disaster, or unless one is waiting to be retrofitted.

At the center of every Gammill machine is a set of hydrodynamically lubricated journal bearings, with clear, non-staining oil delivered by a network of little hoses. Industrial equipment since the 1880s has relied on this type of bearing because of its durability, and this is an important part of why Gammill machines are widely hailed as "workhorse" machines.

What does wear out over decades is everything electrical. Electrical includes motors, electronics, controls, and wiring. So what should your grandchildren do, when the machine they inherited has crusty old wires? Only Gammill machines have Retrofits available. A retrofit takes a Gammill of any era and installs a new electrical system to bring that older machine current: we reuse the machine and table (because they are essentially eternal), and replace the rest with the complete electrical and electronic system of any current model.

Every Gammill built since 1997 is still in service today. This is possible because of retrofitting. No other longarm manufacturer offers retrofitting. When other brands of longarm machines get old, they get thrown away - but not Gammill. It's also why pre-owned Gammills hold the highest resale value in the longarm industry.

03The Table

The table is half the machine.

A great camera in shaky hands isn't going to capture a crisp photo. A great machine on a flexing or shaking table isn't going to stitch accurately either. Every Gammill machine comes on a foundation built for commercial use - welded tube steel, four points of contact, truly level.

Most longarm tables are flat steel, shipped sectional and bolted together at the buyer's house. They cost less to build and ship easier - but they also flex, and make accurate quilting difficult or impossible. A Gammill table is welded from tube steel, one piece, the full length of the table.

When a longarm table flexes, the bars holding the quilt bow in the center. The quilt ends up tight on the edges and unacceptably loose in the middle - uneven tension, the kind of flaws a show judge or a paying customer notices first.

Even a well-built table can be difficult to quilt on if it's not leveled. No ordinary floor is level enough for a longarm machine, so leveling feet are included. Most other longarm tables sag in the middle, so extra legs are added - but six or more leveling feet make it hard to get the machine to glide nicely. A Gammill, even at 14 feet, sits on just four feet to level. One time. Done.

  • Welded tube steel table - one solid piece, not separate sections
  • Just four leveling feet, even on the 14' table - simple to level
  • Breeze Track with self-adjusting wheels - silky-smooth glide
  • 12' GS6 Professional standard for 22/26/30" machines (10' or 14' custom); 10' or 12' Home Pro Table for 18" machines

04Stitch Quality

The least-fussy longarm you can buy.

Stitch quality isn't defined by stitch regulation or motor horsepower. It's about tension that stays put and a machine that moves effortlessly, as if it was reading your mind. Who wants to be ripping out stitching and inventing expletives?

Every modern longarm has stitch regulation and several different stitching modes. Of course the Gammill has the most functional and useful design - but let's not miss the forest for the trees. The two things that actually determine whether your stitching is satisfactory are tension stability and smooth movement.

Tension stability has always been difficult for longarm quilters. Most longarms use the same basic tension design as a domestic sewing machine - but longarm quilting is more demanding, and "okay" tension isn't going to satisfy you. In the earliest days of longarming, quilters noticed loops appearing on the back of the quilt after long days, especially on hot afternoons. Ken Gammill set his inventive mind to the problem and figured out (and patented) two exclusive mechanisms. Thirty years later, Intermittent Tension and Automatic Thread Escapement have come to define perfect stitching in the longarm world. Those dreaded afternoon loops? Unknown to Gammill owners.

Smooth movement is the downstream effect of everything you've already read - the investment-cast aluminum chassis, the welded tube-steel table, the Breeze Track system. The machine goes effortlessly where you want it to.

  • Intermittent Tension separates tension from the checkspring and clamps the top thread at key moments to prevent loops, eyelashes, and railroad tracks
  • Automatic Thread Escapement keeps tension steady through temperature & humidity changes by opening a small "door" for the thread to slip through
  • M-class large-capacity bobbin - about 2× standard capacity, fewer thread changes, with no stitch-quality penalty thanks to the patents above
  • The least-fussy tension in the industry - no accursed loops on the back
  • Smooth movement - downstream effect of the build quality of the machine chassis, table, and Breeze Track

*Intermittent Tension, Automatic Thread Escapement, no timing belts, and the professional table are not available on 18-inch machines. 18" machines include a Home Pro Table.

We meet you at the front door.

Some longarm manufacturers drop a crate on your porch and say good luck. Gammill doesn't. 100% of our machines are delivered and set up in your home by Gammill technicians whose only job is to install Gammill machines correctly.

A Gammill machine isn't shipped to your driveway in a box for you to assemble. It's installed in your home - by a professional Gammill delivery technician - as part of the price of the machine. They arrive on schedule, bring out your old machine if you have one, and stay until your Gammill is in your space, level, calibrated, and ready to stitch.
