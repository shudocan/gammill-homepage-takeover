# STEP 3 · THE TABLE  (`03-table-frame.html`)
Hero image: assets/web/step3-a.jpg  · Has video testimonial

---

NAV PILL  [[s03.nav]]
Why Gammill

KICKER  [[s03.kicker]]
Why Gammill - Chapter 3

HEADLINE  [[s03.h1]]
The table is half<br>the machine.

LEDE  [[s03.lede]]
A great camera in shaky hands isn't going to capture a crisp photo. A great machine on a flexing or shaking table isn't going to stitch accurately either. Every Gammill machine comes on a foundation built for commercial use - welded tube steel, four points of contact, truly level.

BODY KICKER  [[s03.body.kicker]]
Why the foundation matters

BODY HEADLINE  [[s03.body.h2]]
Tube steel. Welded. Four feet on the floor.

BODY · OPENING PARAGRAPH (drop-cap on first letter)  [[s03.body.p1]]
Most longarm tables are built from flat steel and shipped sectional - bolted together at the buyer's house so they fit in a UPS-friendly box. Sections cost less to build and they ship easier. But they also flex, and make accurate quilting difficult or impossible. A Gammill table is welded from tube steel, one piece, the full length of the table. Tube steel is dramatically stiffer than flat steel. Welded is dramatically stiffer than bolted. The result behaves like a single rigid object.

BODY · PARAGRAPH 2  [[s03.body.p2]]
Why this matters: when a longarm table flexes, the bars holding the quilt bow in the center. The quilt ends up tight on the edges and unacceptably loose in the middle. That looseness is the visible signature of a flexing table - uneven tension, the kind of flaws a show judge or a paying customer notices first.

BODY · PARAGRAPH 3  [[s03.body.p3]]
Even a well-built table can be difficult to quilt on if it's not leveled. No ordinary floor is level enough for a longarm machine, so leveling feet are included. Most other longarm tables sag in the middle, so extra legs and feet are added for support - but with six or more leveling feet it can be extremely difficult to get the machine to glide nicely. A Gammill frame, even at the maximum length of 14 feet, uses just **four** feet to level. And Breeze Track, included on every Gammill, self-adjusts as the head moves, so the glide stays silky from one end of the table to the other.

BULLETS  [[s03.body.bullets]]
- Welded tube steel table - one solid piece, not separate sections
- Just four leveling feet, even on the 14' table - simple to level
- Breeze Track with self-adjusting wheels - a silky-smooth glide
- **12' GS6 Professional table** standard for 22", 26", and 30" machines; custom 10' or 14' available. 18" machines come on a **10' or 12' Home Pro Table** (custom lengths available).

NAV PILL  [[s04.nav]]
Why Gammill

KICKER  [[s04.kicker]]
Why Gammill - Chapter 4

HEADLINE  [[s04.h1]]
The least-fussy<br>longarm you can buy.

LEDE  [[s04.lede]]
Stitch quality isn't defined by stitch regulation or motor horsepower. It's about **tension that stays put** and a machine that moves **effortlessly**, as if it was reading your mind. Who wants to be ripping out stitching and inventing expletives?

BODY KICKER  [[s04.body.kicker]]
What stitch quality is actually about

BODY HEADLINE  [[s04.body.h2]]
Tension that stays put. Movement that doesn't fight back.

BODY · OPENING PARAGRAPH (drop-cap on first letter)  [[s04.body.p1]]
Every modern longarm has stitch regulation and several different stitching modes. Of course the Gammill has the most functional and useful design - but let's not miss the forest for the trees. The two things that actually determine whether your stitching is satisfactory are **tension stability** and **smooth movement.**

BODY · PARAGRAPH 2  [[s04.body.p2]]
Tension stability has always been difficult for longarm quilters. Most longarms use the same basic tension design as a domestic sewing machine - but longarm quilting is more demanding, and "okay" tension isn't going to satisfy you. In the earliest days of longarming, quilters noticed loops appearing on the back of the quilt after long days, especially on hot afternoons. Ken Gammill set his inventive mind to the problem and figured out (and patented) two exclusive mechanisms. Thirty years later, **Intermittent Tension** and **Automatic Thread Escapement** have come to define perfect stitching in the longarm world. Those dreaded afternoon loops? Unknown to Gammill owners.

BODY · PARAGRAPH 3  [[s04.body.p3]]
Smooth movement is the downstream effect of everything you've already read - the investment-cast aluminum chassis, the welded tube-steel table, the Breeze Track system. The machine goes effortlessly where you want it to.

BULLETS  [[s04.body.bullets]]
- **Intermittent Tension** separates tension from the checkspring and clamps the top thread at key moments to prevent loops, eyelashes, and railroad tracks
- **Automatic Thread Escapement** keeps tension steady through temperature and humidity changes by opening a small "door" for the thread to slip through
- **M-class large-capacity bobbin** - about 2× standard capacity, fewer thread changes, with no stitch-quality penalty thanks to the patents above
- The least-fussy tension in the industry - no accursed loops on the back
- Smooth movement - downstream effect of the build quality of the machine chassis, table, and Breeze Track
- Four stitch modes included (Stitch Regulate, Coast, Constant Speed, Baste) - table stakes, but standard

APPLICABILITY SUBTEXT (small, muted, sits below the bullets)  [[s04.body.applicability]]
*Intermittent Tension, Automatic Thread Escapement, no timing belts, and the professional table are not available on 18-inch machines. 18" machines include a Home Pro Table.
