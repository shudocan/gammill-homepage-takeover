The numbers, for the curious

If quilting becomes more than a hobby, here's what it can do.

A computerized Gammill at part-time pace - 20 hours a week, one quilt a day at a $150 average ticket - generates roughly $3,000/mo in revenue. Subtract financing (~$1,100/mo on the Affordable Financing Program) and the basics (power, insurance, maintenance, ~$150/mo), and the machine pays itself off while putting about $1,750/mo in your pocket. Once the loan is gone, that's $2,850/mo - on 20 hours a week.

If quilting becomes the work and not the side hustle: an established computerized longarmer finishes two to three queen-size edge-to-edge tops a day. At normal market rates that's $500-$600 per day, single machine. Quilt shop owners who add a longarm report fabric sales 2-3x after - because customers who drop off tops walk through the fabric to do it.

The monthly payment is not the real cost.

Here's what most longarm pitches miss. Buy a $35,000 car on a 7-year loan, drive it for 7 years, sell it for $14,000 - your actual cost was $21,000, or $3,000/year. The rest you got back. A Gammill works the same way. Depreciation flatlines at around $1,000/year after year 10, so the real cost of a Gammill across decades is a tiny number - and any income from customer quilting makes it negative.
