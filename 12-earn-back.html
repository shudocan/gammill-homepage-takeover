Why Gammill - Chapter 12

Do you want to earn back your investment?

Many Gammill owners are hobbyists - they buy a longarm for love, not money. But many also discover their Gammill can earn itself back, sometimes within 18 months, by quilting tops for other people. It's optional. But... the math works.

The numbers, for the curious

If quilting becomes more than a hobby, here's what it can do.

A computerized Gammill at part-time pace - 20 hours a week, one quilt a day at a $150 average ticket - generates roughly $3,000/mo in revenue. Here's where that goes:

Illustrative numbers at 20 hr/week.

If quilting becomes your primary work and not just a side hustle: an established Statler longarmer finishes two to three queen-size edge-to-edge tops a day. At normal market rates that's $500-$600 per day, from a single machine. (An Ascend finishes 3-5 a day.)

Quilt shop owners who add a Statler report fabric sales 2-3x afterwards - because customers who only came in to drop off a quilt top are walking past the fabric to get to the drop-off counter.

Real Gammill business owners on what changed for them.

The monthly payment is not the real cost.

Most longarm pitches obsess over the monthly payment. The real cost of any major purchase is depreciation - what you spent, minus what you got back when you sold it.

$35,000 Car, owned 7 years then sold

Half the annual cost - on a machine that costs more upfront.

And it gets better with time. Gammill depreciation flatlines at around $1,000/year after year 10, so the real cost across decades drops even lower. Any income from customer quilting makes the real cost negative.

Earnings and financial details in this chapter are easily verified by listening to (or watching) the Quilting Business Success podcast and by using the Business Plan Tool at gammill.com to generate your own plan based on your local market, the machine you choose, how many hours per week you're willing to work, and how you price your services.
